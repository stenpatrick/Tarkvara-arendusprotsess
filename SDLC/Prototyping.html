Prototype

Prototype Model is a Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) model that involves creating an early, rudimentary version of a system, software, or interface for demonstration and evaluation purposes.
It focuses on quickly building a prototype to showcase the basic functionality and design of the final product.

Advantages

  • 1. Rapid Prototyping: Prototype Model allows for rapid prototyping, enabling stakeholders to visualize and interact with the product early in the development process.

  • 2. Early Feedback: Stakeholders can provide early feedback on the prototype, allowing for iteration and refinement of the final product.

  • 3. Clear Requirements: Developing a prototype helps clarify and refine the requirements of the final product through hands-on exploration.

  • 4. Risk Mitigation: By identifying and addressing potential issues early in the development cycle, Prototype Model helps mitigate risks associated with the final product.

  • 5. User Involvement: Involving users in the prototyping process enhances user satisfaction and ensures that the final product meets their needs and expectations.

  • 6. Cost-Effective: Developing a prototype can be cost-effective as it helps identify and rectify design flaws and requirements issues early in the development process, reducing rework costs later on.

Disadvantages

  • 1. Limited Functionality: Prototypes may lack full functionality and may not accurately represent the final product, leading to potential misunderstandings.

  • 2. Time Constraints: Developing a prototype requires additional time and resources upfront, potentially delaying the overall development process.

  • 3. Scope Creep: Stakeholders may request additional features or changes during the prototyping phase, leading to scope creep and increased project complexity.

  • 4. Maintenance Overhead: Maintaining multiple prototypes and ensuring consistency between them can create additional overhead for development teams.

  • 5. Misleading Expectations: Stakeholders may form unrealistic expectations based on the prototype, leading to disappointment if the final product differs significantly.

  • 6. Limited Scalability: Prototype Model may not be suitable for large-scale projects or projects requiring extensive scalability and robustness.
